When IUL Is Oversold

🔥 Heat 5/5🤫 How quiet 4/5

Indexed universal life is often illustrated with optimistic returns, but caps and participation rates can change over time, the internal cost of insurance rises as you age, and the policy has to be actively managed to stay healthy.

None of that makes IUL bad. For the right person, properly structured and funded, it can be a genuinely useful tool. The disappointment usually comes when someone is handed a glossy best-case illustration as if it were a guarantee, and nobody walks them through what happens if returns come in lower.

Red flags to watch for

  • An illustration presented as if it is guaranteed
  • Heavy focus on the best-case column
  • No discussion of lower-return scenarios
  • Pressure to fund at the bare minimum

Questions worth asking

What return assumptions are in this illustration?
What happens if returns are lower than shown?
What are the policy's expenses and cost of insurance?
What is the alternative, and how do they compare?

What consumers should know

IULs can be excellent tools or real disappointments. The difference is honest assumptions and active management.
