Why Every Drug Ad Ends With 'Ask Your Doctor'

🔥 Heat 5/5🤫 How quiet 4/5

Every drug ad closes the same way: ask your doctor about it. It sounds like empowerment, but it functions as a sales funnel that ends in an exam room. The United States is one of only two countries that allow this kind of direct-to-consumer drug advertising.

Research has found that patients who ask for an advertised drug are far more likely to leave with that exact prescription, and the ad boom drove a large share of the rise in drug spending. The effect is that the viewer becomes the one nudging their own doctor toward the newest, priciest option.

Red flags to watch for

  • An ad telling you which drug to request
  • Pressure to ask for something by brand name
  • A newest, priciest option presented as default
  • No mention of alternatives or generics

Questions worth asking

Is there an equally effective generic?
What are the alternatives, including non-drug options?
Why this specific medication for me?
What does the evidence actually show?

What consumers should know

'Ask your doctor' is marketing language. Bring questions, not a brand name, into the exam room.
